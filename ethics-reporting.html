Introduction to Ethics & Reporting

Using AI responsibly does not end when you have your output. This page covers the ethical considerations that arise when you use AI-generated analysis in real-world reports and decisions — and how to navigate them with confidence.

In this section you will learn how to…

  • Decide when and how to disclose that AI was used
  • Respond when your AI output is questioned or challenged
  • Recognise and address bias in AI outputs
  • Handle situations where colleagues or stakeholders distrust AI
  • Maintain your own responsibility as the author of any report

1. You are always responsible


When you produce a report, briefing, or analysis — even if AI helped you write it — you are responsible for its content.


AI does not have judgement, values, or accountability. It generates text based on patterns. You are the one who decides what to include, what to leave out, how to frame findings, and who the work goes to. That responsibility cannot be delegated to a tool.


This means:

  • Always read AI outputs in full before sharing them.
  • Always check key facts against your original material.
  • Always apply your own knowledge of the community or context.
  • Be prepared to explain and defend every claim in your report.

2. When and how to disclose AI use


As AI becomes more common in research and reporting, transparency about how it was used is increasingly expected — and in some contexts, required.

A useful starting point: always disclose the use of AI!


What good disclosure looks like

You do not need to write a lengthy technical explanation. A short, plain-language statement in your approach or acknowledgements section is usually enough.


For example:

  • Qualitative responses were analysed using [name of the tool] to identify initial themes. All AI-generated analysis was reviewed and edited by [name of the team of people reviewing the output]. The prompts used in this process are available on request.

If AI was used only for minor tasks, such as improving the grammar of a draft or formatting a table, you may choose not to disclose this formally, though you should use your judgement based on the context and the expectations of your audience.

3. Ethical scenarios

The scenarios below illustrate situations you might encounter and suggest how to handle them.


Scenario: You have produced an impact report using AI analysis of community consultation responses. It will be presented to councillors to support a budget decision.


What to do: Be especially thorough in your review. Cross-check every key finding against the original responses. Disclose clearly that AI was used for initial analysis and that the findings were verified by the research team. Be prepared to share the original data if asked. Never present AI-generated text as if it were a direct quote from participants.

Scenario:A stakeholder or colleague says they do not trust the report because AI was involved and argues the findings may be biased or inaccurate.


What to do: Stay calm and take the challenge seriously. Explain what AI was used for and what it was not used for. Show that you have reviewed and validated the outputs against the original material. If they identify a specific concern, investigate it — they may be right. Being open about the process, including its limitations, builds more trust than being defensive.

Scenario:Someone in your organisation does not want AI used in your work. Their concerns may be about privacy, environmental impact, the tech industry, or values more broadly.


What to do: Listen to their concerns and take them seriously — they may be well-founded. Discuss whether and how AI will be used before you start, not after. Consider whether it is possible to limit AI use to specific tasks they are comfortable with (e.g. drafting rather than analysis). Document the discussion and any agreements. In some cases, the right answer may be not to use AI for a particular piece of work.

Scenario: The AI's thematic analysis seems to miss or flatten important experiences, particularly from marginalised or less vocal participants.


What to do: Go back to the original material. Look specifically at the responses that seem underrepresented. Write additional analysis by hand for those voices. Consider framing this explicitly in your report: 'The AI analysis identified the following majority themes. A separate review identified the following concerns raised by a smaller number of participants, which the team considered significant.' Quantitative proportions can be misleading when working with small or diverse communities.

Scenario: The AI has produced a summary sentence that paraphrases participant responses, but it is not a direct quote. You are unsure how to present it.


What to do: Never present AI-generated text as a direct participant quote. If something is a paraphrase or synthesis produced by AI, present it as such — e.g. 'Participants expressed concerns about access, including issues related to transport and opening times.' If you want to use a direct quote, go back to the original transcript and find one.
