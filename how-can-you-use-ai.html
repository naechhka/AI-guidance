Introduction to Using AI

This page takes you through the process of using an AI tool step by step — from uploading your material to producing a final output. It also gives you ready-made prompt examples for the most common tasks.

In this section you will learn how to…

  • Choose the right AI tool for your task
  • Upload or paste your material
  • Write effective prompts
  • Review and refine AI outputs
  • Produce a final report or communication

1. Tools you can use


There are many AI tools available, and they all work in a broadly similar way. Below are some of the most common ones relevant to community and third-sector research work — but this is not an exhaustive list, and new tools are emerging all the time.

2. Step-by-step guide

Before you open an AI tool, make sure your material is ready:

  • Text documents should be in a readable format (Word, PDF, or plain text).
  • Voice recordings should be transcribed into text.
  • Personal information should be anonymised.

Open the tool of your choice and start a new chat or notebook. Each conversation starts fresh — the AI does not remember previous sessions unless you are using a tool with memory enabled.

Most tools allow you to attach a file (PDF, Word document, or spreadsheet) or paste text directly into the chat.

A prompt is the instruction you give the AI. The clearer your prompt, the better the output. A good prompt usually includes:

  • What the AI should do — e.g. 'Summarise', 'Identify themes', 'Write a report'
  • What the material is — e.g. 'These are responses from a community consultation'
  • Who the output is for — e.g. 'This will be read by council officers'
  • Any constraints — e.g. 'Keep it under 500 words' or 'Use plain language'

See the prompt cards below for ready-made examples you can copy and adapt.

Read the AI's response carefully. Ask yourself:

  • Is this accurate? Does it match what the source material actually says?
  • Is anything missing? Has the AI left out important voices or concerns?
  • Is the tone right for the intended audience?
  • Are there any signs of bias or oversimplification?

You do not have to accept the first output. You can ask the AI to:

  • 'Make this shorter / longer'
  • 'Use simpler language'
  • 'Add more detail on the theme of [topic]'
  • 'Rewrite this for a non-specialist audience'
  • 'Check whether you have missed any concerns raised by participants'

Think of it as a conversation, not a single command. Multiple rounds of refinement usually produce much better results than trying to write the perfect prompt first time.

Once you are happy with the output, copy it into your own document to format and finalise. Some AI tools will also allow you to export the answer in a format of your choice (e.g., word document, PDF, etc). Always review the full text one more time before sharing. The AI's version is a starting point, not a finished product.

3. Prompt examples

When to use: You have a set of survey responses, interview transcripts, or consultation notes and want to identify the main themes.


Example prompt:

I have uploaded [number] responses from a community consultation about [topic]. Please read through them and identify the main themes that emerge. For each theme, provide a short description and two or three example quotes that illustrate it.


What you will get: A structured list of themes with descriptions and supporting quotes. Good starting point for a report or presentation.

When to use: You want to understand the overall mood or feeling in a set of responses — positive, negative, mixed, or neutral.


Example prompt:

I have uploaded responses from [number] community members about [topic]. Please analyse the overall sentiment of these responses. Identify what proportion are broadly positive, negative, or mixed. For each category, give a short summary of what is driving that sentiment and include one or two illustrative quotes.


What you will get: A sentiment summary with proportions and supporting examples. Useful for impact reporting or councillor briefings.

When to use: You have a lengthy report, policy document, or meeting transcript and want a shorter version.


Example prompt:

Please summarise the attached document in [X] words or fewer. The summary is for [audience — e.g. 'a community group with no specialist knowledge' / 'senior managers']. Focus on the key findings and any recommended actions. Use plain, accessible language and avoid jargon.


What you will get: A summary matched to the length and audience you specified.

When to use: You want the AI to find publicly available data or reports to support your work. (Note: not all tools can search the web — ChatGPT with browsing enabled and Gemini can.)


Example prompt:

Please search for recent statistics or reports on [topic] in [location or country]. I am looking for information published in the last [X] years. Summarise the most relevant findings and include links to the original sources.


What you will get: A summary of publicly available information with source links for verification. Always check the sources yourself.

When to use: You have data in a spreadsheet or table and want to visualise it. You can paste the data directly into the prompt.


Example prompt:

Here is data from [briefly provide some details]: [paste table or describe the data]. Please suggest the most appropriate type of chart for this data, explain why, and then create it. The chart is for [audience]. Make sure any labels are clear and accessible.


What you will get: A suggested chart type with rationale, followed by the chart itself. You can then export or screenshot it for your report.

When to use: You want to produce a first draft of an impact report, briefing, leaflet, or email.


Example prompt:

Using the analysis and themes from our conversation so far [or: using the attached document], please draft a [type of document — e.g. two-page impact report / briefing for councillors / community newsletter]. The audience is [describe]. The tone should be [e.g. formal / accessible / motivating]. Include a short introduction, key findings, and a conclusion. Keep it under [word limit].


What you will get: A complete first draft that you can edit, expand, and personalise before sharing.

Prompting Tips:

DO

  • Be specific about what you want — length, format, audience, tone
  • Provide context: tell the AI who you are and what the material is
  • Break complex tasks into smaller steps
  • Ask follow-up questions to refine the output
  • Tell the AI when something is wrong — it can correct itself

DON'T

  • Use vague instructions like 'analyse this' with no further detail
  • Upload very large files without checking they are within the tool's limit
  • Treat the first output as final — always review and refine
  • Assume the AI remembers previous conversations
  • Upload personally identifiable information
