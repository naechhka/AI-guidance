Introduction to Troubleshooting

Something not working as expected? This page covers the most common problems people run into when using AI tools — and what to do about them.

Common problems and what to do


PROMPTS AND OUTPUTS

Rephrase your prompt and be more specific. Add context: what the material is, and what you want the output to look like. For example, instead of 'Analyse this document', try: 'I have uploaded a set of consultation responses from community members about local transport. Please identify the three most common concerns and give one example quote for each.' If the AI is still off track, start a new conversation, sometimes a fresh start is more effective than trying to correct a confused thread.

Ask the AI to shorten it. You can say: 'Please rewrite this in [X] words or fewer', or 'Give me a shorter version, keeping only the most important points.' You can also ask for a bullet-point summary: 'Summarise the above in five bullet points.' If length is consistently a problem, add a word limit to your original prompt.

Ask the AI to expand. For example: 'Please add more detail to the section on [theme]', or 'Can you give more examples from the original material to support each theme?' You can also ask: 'Are there any important points you left out?' Sometimes the AI will flag things it abbreviated.

Start a new conversation. Long threads can cause the AI to loop or become less coherent. Before starting again, note down what worked and what did not, and write a cleaner, more focused prompt for the new session.

Give the AI a style example. Paste in a paragraph of your own writing and say: 'Please match this style and tone in your response.' You can also use instructions like: 'Write in plain, conversational language, don’t incorporate jargon' or 'Use a formal but accessible tone, suitable for a public report.'

ACCURACY AND TRUST

This is one of the most important risks of using AI. If you spot something that does not match your source material, do not include it. Correct the AI by saying: 'This quote does not appear in the material I provided. Please only use information from the documents I have given you.' Then go back and check the rest of the output more carefully. Consider adding this instruction to all your prompts: 'Only use information from the documents I have provided. Do not add external facts, statistics, or quotes.'

Ask the AI to look specifically for underrepresented voices: 'Are there any perspectives in the material that are less well represented in your analysis? Please add a section on minority or dissenting views.' Also review the output against the original yourself, paying attention to whether certain types of responses — perhaps from smaller or more marginalised groups — have been underweighted. Adjust or add to the analysis by hand if needed.

TECHNICAL AND PRACTICAL ISSUES

Try splitting the document into sections and uploading them separately, analysing each one in turn. For very large collections of documents, consider using NotebookLM, which is designed to handle multiple sources. Alternatively, copy and paste the most relevant sections of text rather than uploading the full file

Some PDFs are scanned images rather than text-based documents. The AI cannot read text in image-only PDFs. You will need to convert the PDF to text first using an OCR (optical character recognition) tool — Microsoft Word can do this if you open the PDF directly. Once converted, save as a Word document and try again.

Most AI tools cannot visit URLs directly. Instead of pasting a link, paste the relevant text from the page or document into the chat. For web content, you can copy and paste the text from your browser. For Google Docs, use File → Download → Plain Text and upload the downloaded file.

Most AI tools save your conversation history automatically — look for a sidebar or history panel. If you cannot find it, check your account settings. For important work, always copy the AI's output into your own document as you go, rather than relying on the AI's conversation history.

WORKING WITH OTHERS

Take the concern seriously and invite them to review the original material alongside the AI's analysis. Show them your process — the prompts you used, what you reviewed, what you changed. If their concern is about a specific claim, check it together. In some cases, the right response is to redo part of the analysis manually rather than relying on AI. Trust is more important than efficiency.

Start a conversation about it. Raise the question with your manager or board. In the meantime, use AI only for tasks where the data is non-sensitive and you are confident about consent. Document what you are doing and why. If your organisation does adopt a policy, make sure it covers: which tools are approved, what data can be processed, how AI use should be disclosed, and who is responsible for reviewing outputs.

Start with one of the free options — ChatGPT or Gemini are good starting points for general tasks. If you are working primarily with documents, NotebookLM is worth exploring. Try the same task in more than one tool and compare the outputs. You do not need to commit to one tool permanently — different tasks may suit different tools.

Glossary


A quick reference for the key terms used on this website.


AI (Artificial Intelligence): On this website, we use 'AI' to refer to large language model tools such as ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and NotebookLM.


LLM (Large Language Model):A type of AI trained on large amounts of text. It generates responses by predicting what text should come next based on patterns in its training data.


Prompt: The instruction or question you type into an AI tool. The quality of your prompt strongly influences the quality of the output.


Output: The text the AI produces in response to your prompt.


Hallucination: When an AI generates something that sounds plausible but is factually incorrect or not present in the source material. Always check AI outputs against your original data.


Thematic analysis: Identifying recurring themes or patterns across a set of responses or texts.


Sentiment analysis: Assessing the overall mood or feeling in a set of responses — positive, negative, neutral, or mixed.


Transcription: Converting audio recordings into written text, so they can be analysed by an AI tool.


Anonymisation Removing or replacing personal details (names, locations, identifying information) from a document before processing.


OCR (Optical Character Recognition): Technology that converts scanned images of text into editable, searchable text. Needed when a PDF is image-based rather than text-based.


Bias: Systematic errors or distortions in AI outputs that often reflect imbalances or assumptions in the training data. Cannot be fully eliminated, but can be identified and managed.


Context window: The maximum amount of text an AI can process in a single conversation. Very long documents may exceed this limit.
