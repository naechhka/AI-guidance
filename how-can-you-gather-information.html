Introduction to gathering information

Before you use an AI tool to analyse anything, you need to have gathered the right material. This page covers what kinds of information you can feed into AI, and how to do this responsibly — especially when working with communities.

In this section you will learn how to...

Identify what types of documents and data AI can work with

Collect community feedback and qualitative data in a way that is ready for AI analysis

Handle consent, privacy, and data ethics from the very start

1. What can you feed into AI?

AI tools can work with a wide variety of inputs. Here are the most common types used:

Documents & text

Audio & voice recordings

Images & visual documents

Spreadsheets & data files

2. Practical limits to be aware of


AI tools are not unlimited. Here are some things to keep in mind when preparing your materials:

Text limits

Most tools have a limit on how much text you can provide at once. Very long documents may need to be split into sections.

Quality matters

The quality of the AI's analysis depends on the quality of the material you provide. Messy, inconsistent, or very informal text will produce less reliable results.

Language limitations

AI works best when the material is in a language it has been extensively trained on — usually English. Results in other languages can be less reliable.

Handwriting

Handwriting is difficult for AI to read unless it has been transcribed into text first.

3. Is my data private?


When you upload a document or paste text into an AI tool, that information is sent to the company that runs the tool. Different tools have different privacy policies — some may use your inputs to improve their models; others offer confidential modes for professional or organisational use.

As a general rule:

Do not upload documents containing personal or sensitive information unless you have checked the tool's privacy policy. Do not paste in names, addresses, health data, or other identifying details about the people you work with. Always obtain consent from community members before processing their words, recordings, or responses using an AI tool. Tell participants clearly how their data will be used and by whom.

4. Consent & Policy


Before you collect any information that you plan to analyse using AI, you must think carefully about consent and data handling.


  • Explain how data will be used — including that it may be processed using an AI tool. Be specific: name the tool if possible.
  • Obtain informed consent — participants should actively agree, not just have a notice in small print.
  • Explain who will have access to the data — and whether it will be stored in a third-party tool.
  • Clarify whether responses will be anonymised before analysis — and follow through on this.
  • Give participants the right to withdraw — and explain what this means in practice.
  • Check your organisation's data protection policy before starting.

    If you are recording interviews or group discussions:


  • Always ask for verbal and written consent before pressing record.
  • Explain that the recording may be transcribed and that the transcript may be processed by an AI tool.
  • Transcribe recordings as soon as possible and store them securely.
  • Consider whether to anonymise names and identifying details in the transcript before uploading to an AI tool.
  • Delete recordings once transcribed, unless participants have consented to their storage.

    If your research involves communities with protected characteristics — such as age, disability, ethnicity, religion, or gender — take extra care:


  • AI tools may produce outputs that reflect biases from their training data. These biases can disproportionately affect marginalised groups.
  • Do not rely on AI alone to interpret responses from these communities. Human review by someone with relevant expertise or lived experience is essential.
  • Be particularly careful about anonymisation — small communities can be identifiable even without names.

    Before uploading transcripts, survey responses, or other documents to an AI tool, consider replacing names and identifying details with neutral labels:


  • Replace names with 'Participant 1', 'Participant 2', etc.
  • Replace place names with 'local area' or a code.
  • Remove any details — job titles, dates, organisations — that could identify individuals.

5. Use case: Collecting community feedback


If you are designing a survey, interview guide, or community consultation specifically to feed into AI analysis, the way you frame your questions matters.


Questions that work well:

  • Open questions that invite people to explain in their own words — e.g. 'What has changed in your community in the last year?'
  • Questions about specific experiences — e.g. 'Can you describe a time when you used this service?'
  • Questions about impact — e.g. 'What difference has this project made to you?'
  • Questions that allow for complexity and nuance

Questions that work less well:

  • Yes/no or multiple-choice only questions (AI has little to analyse)
  • Very leading questions that push people towards a particular answer
  • Questions that are ambiguous or double-barrelled
  • Questions that mix multiple topics in one

Useful tips when collecting feedback

  • Collect enough material — AI analysis of fewer than five or six responses may not be meaningful.
  • Keep original responses intact — do not paraphrase people's words before uploading. AI works best with verbatim responses.
  • Note context separately — if you know something important about a respondent (e.g. they are a carer, or they live in a specific area), record this separately so you can refer to it when interpreting AI outputs.
  • Use consistent formats — if collecting responses from multiple sources, try to use the same structure so the AI can compare across them.
